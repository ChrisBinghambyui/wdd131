IMPERIAL ENGLAND - 500 Common Word Translations
Fantasy Language: Imperial England
Linguistic roots: 50% Old/Middle English (equal blend, 5th-15th c.)
+ 50% borrowed/absorbed/conquered from 19 other Durgeth languages
Stylization: Fantasy-leaning. Imperial England feels like a dominant
tongue that has swallowed vocabulary from every culture it has touched.

NATURE OF BORROWING:
  CONQUEST borrowings (harder absorption, sometimes clipped/anglicized):
    Bovari Scythian, Naukin Mongolian, Stoneguard Toltec,
    Veildrift Slavic, Verdathi Aboriginal, Sylvarirum Navajo
  TRADE borrowings (softer, more equal assimilation):
    Solarirum French, Sunblade Greek, Gorirum Persian,
    Vethanirum Catalan, Apisveldir Byzantine, Apiskeld Sumerian
  ABSORBED borrowings (place-names, nature words, concepts):
    Ashirum Icelandic, Nordal Russian, Ashveld Swahili,
    Murrak Tasmanian-Etruscan, Arantza Basque, Apisdren Bhutan,
    Kreln Welsh-Polynesian

KEY OLD/MIDDLE ENGLISH ROOTS:
  OE: þe/se (the), and/ond (and), ne/na (not), on/an (on/at),
  of (of), mid (with), to (to), gif (if), forþan (because),
  beon/wesan (be), habban (have), don (do), secgan (say), geseon (see),
  gan (go), cuman (come), witan (know), gifan (give), niman (take),
  man/guma (man), wif (woman), cild (child), cyning (king),
  eorþe (earth), fyr (fire), wæter (water), heofon (sky/heaven),
  dæg (day), mona (moon), steorra (star), god (good), micel/mycel (great)
  ME: the, and, nat/not, on, of, with, to, if, for, because,
  been, have, do, say, see, go, come, know, give, take,
  man, woman, child, king, earth, fire, water, heven, day,
  moon, sterre, good, grete, true, world, time, place, way

SUFFIX/AFFIX SYSTEM FOR IMPERIAL ENGLAND:
  -en  = OE/ME infinitive/past participle marker
  -ish = OE/ME adjectival suffix (absorbed as quality marker)
  -wyn = OE/ME joy/delight (used as positive noun suffix)
  -ward = OE direction suffix (used for directional/place words)
  -dom  = OE realm/state suffix
  -craft = OE skill/art suffix
  Borrowed suffixes retained: -vel (Solarirum), -ara (Gorirum),
  -tli (Stoneguard, conquest-clipped to -tl), -vrath (Veildrift),
  -lara (Murrak, softened to -lar in Imperial)

PHONETIC GUIDE FOR IMPERIAL ENGLAND

Old English sounds preserved:
  þ   = voiceless dental fricative (as in "thin") — the thorn
  ð   = voiced dental fricative (as in "the") — the eth
  ā/ē/ī/ō/ū = long vowels (marked with macron)
  sc  = /sh/ sound (scip = ship, scēodan = shadow)
  cg  = /dj/ sound (ecg = edge)
  hw  = voiceless w (hwæt, hwīt, hwēol)
  ċ   = /ch/ sound before front vowels (cild = child)
  Many OE words: directly attested in Beowulf, Anglo-Saxon Chronicle,
  Alfred's translations, Ælfric's homilies

Middle English sounds:
  -en = infinitive/participle ending (geten, writen)
  -vel = French trade suffix (integrated from Solarirum)
  -dom = OE realm/state (used more broadly in ME)
  -craft = OE skill/art (heavily used in Imperial compound words)
  -wyn = OE joy/pleasure (used as positive abstract suffix)
  -ward = OE direction (used for positional/place concepts)

Borrowed phonemes that survive in Imperial:
  θ   = from Murrak (the dental fricative — merges with OE þ/ð)
