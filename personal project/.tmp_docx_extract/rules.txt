A Tabletop Roleplaying Game
of Swords, Sorcery & Boundless Horizons

Core Rulebook  ·  0.13 Edition
For 2–6 Players
Chapter 1: Introduction
Welcome to Scrolls & Steel — a tabletop roleplaying game of ancient magic, dangerous roads, and characters defined not by the title they hold, but by the life they have lived. You will build a character who grows through practice, forges their own spells, inscribes their own enchantments, and carves their name into a world that does not give it out for free.
If you have played other tabletop roleplaying games, many concepts here will feel familiar: you and your friends sit around a table, you describe what your characters do, and dice determine whether the world cooperates. The differences run deeper, though, and they begin with a single idea — you are what you practice.
What Makes This Game Different
Skills, not classes. There are no rigid class progressions. Optional pre-built archetypes (Chapter 4) bundle skill selections together to get you started quickly, but the underlying system is entirely skill-based. A warrior who spends her evenings reading enchanting texts will, over time, become a warrior-enchanter. The game reflects the life you play, not the box you checked at character creation.
Roll under. All checks use a d100 — two ten-sided dice. Roll equal to or under your effective skill to succeed. Higher skill means more room beneath it. Difficulty shifts your roll, not a pile of bonuses to track.
Magicka as a pool. Spellcasters draw from a finite reserve of Magicka. No spell slots. You spend what you have, rest to recover it, and manage the gap between.
Craft your own magic. Spells and enchanted items are not selected from a fixed list — they are built from learned components. The spell list in Chapter 7 is a foundation, not a ceiling.
Two resource pools. You track Health (how close to death you are) and Fatigue (how tired you are). Both matter in every fight. Running low on Fatigue makes you stumble; running out can kill you as surely as a blade.
The Golden Rule
When rules do not cover a situation, the Game Master makes a ruling and the game moves on. No rulebook can anticipate everything. When in doubt, call for a d100 roll against the most relevant skill.
Chapter 2: Attributes
Every character has eight core Attributes, each rated from 10 to 100. Attributes represent raw natural capacity — the ceiling of how good you can become in related skills, and the source of several derived statistics you will reference constantly during play.
Attributes do not add directly to your rolls. Instead, they cap what your skills can grow to, and feed into the derived numbers (Health, Magicka, Fatigue) that determine how long you survive.
The Eight Attributes
Attribute
What It Governs
Strength (STR)
Physical power. Governs melee damage with heavier weapons, carrying capacity, and feats of raw force.
Endurance (END)
Stamina and toughness. Governs Health points and your capacity to keep fighting after punishment.
Agility (AGI)
Coordination and quickness. Governs turn order, damage with lighter weapons and ranged attacks, and dodge-based defense.
Speed (SPD)
How fast you move. Governs movement in meters per turn.
Intelligence (INT)
Knowledge and analytical thought. Governs Magicka points and how readily you learn new spell effects.
Willpower (WIL)
Mental fortitude. Governs Magicka regeneration and resistance to mind-affecting magic.
Personality (PER)
Presence and charm. Governs social skill checks and the initial attitude of NPCs toward you.
Luck (LCK)
Fortune. Either provides a flat bonus to every roll, or fuels a pool of Luck Dice — see the variant rule below.
Derived Statistics
Calculate these numbers at character creation. Update them when the relevant Attribute changes. During play, you only need to track three live numbers: current HP, current MP, and current FP.
Statistic
Formula
Health Points (HP)
END ÷ 5, round down. +2 per level after 1st.
Magicka Points (MP)
INT ÷ 5, round down. +1 per level after 1st.
Fatigue Points (FP)
(END + STR + AGI + SPD) ÷ 10, round down.
Initiative
AGI ÷ 10, round down. Roll d10 at combat start and add this.
Luck Bonus
LCK ÷ 10, round down. Added to every skill roll (or replaced by Luck Dice — see variant).
Melee Damage Bonus
STR ÷ 20, round down. Added to medium and heavy weapon damage.
Agility Damage Bonus
AGI ÷ 20, round down. Added to light weapon and ranged damage.
Movement Speed
SPD ÷ 2 (round to nearest 5) meters per turn. Minimum 10m.
Carry Weight
STR * 5. Minimum 50.
VARIANT RULE — LUCK POINTS
Instead of a flat Luck Bonus added to every roll, some groups prefer fortune to be a dramatic, sparing resource.
LUCK POINTS POOL: Divide LCK by 30, rounding down. This is the number of Luck Points you receive
per long rest. LCK 30 = 1 point. LCK 60 = 2 points. LCK 90 = 3 points.
SPENDING A LUCK POINT: Declare before or after rolling. The roll automatically succeeds — no check
needed. You may not spend a Luck Point on a roll that has already been declared a critical failure
(natural 98–99); fate has spoken.
RECOVERY: Luck Points refill completely on a long rest. They do not refill on short rests.
This variant makes fortune feel genuinely miraculous — something saved for the moment that truly
matters, rather than a passive number sitting on every roll. Recommended for groups that enjoy high
dramatic stakes and resource tension.
Setting Starting Attributes
Set all Attributes to 40, then distribute 30 extra points however you like. No Attribute may be below 10 or above 65 before racial bonuses; after racial bonuses, the cap is 75 at character creation. Through leveling and Feats, Attributes may be raised up to 100.
For full control: distribute 350 points from scratch instead, with the same floor and ceiling.
Pre-built archetypes (Chapter 4) distribute Attributes for you and let you skip this step.
Chapter 3: Skills
Skills are the most important numbers on your character sheet. There are 22 skills, each rated from 5 to 100. Your governing Attribute sets the ceiling — no skill may exceed the Attribute (or the average of two Attributes) that governs it, except through temporary magical enhancement.
Skills improve through use. The more you practice a skill in play, the more opportunities you get to advance it. This means a character's sheet slowly reshapes itself to match how they are actually played.

Armor Skills and Armor Rating
Your skill in an armor type actively improves its protection. As you become more experienced fighting in a particular armor, you learn how to move within it, angle it against blows, and maintain it for peak effectiveness.

Example: A character wearing Chainmail (base AR 4) with Medium Armor skill 55 has an effective AR of 6 (4 base + 2 skill bonus).
Major, Minor, and Miscellaneous Skills
At character creation, your 21 skills are divided into three tiers. This division is the single most important structural choice you make when building a character from scratch. Pre-built archetypes handle this for you.
Major Skills (choose 5)
The skills that define your character's core identity. Majors start at 40 and advance more quickly. Every 10 increases across your Major Skills triggers a Level Up.
Minor Skills (choose 5)
Serious secondary pursuits. Minors start at 25 and advance at the same rate. Every 20 Minor Skill increases triggers a bonus Level Up.
Miscellaneous Skills (remaining)
Background exposure. Misc skills start at 5, require two meaningful uses per tick, and do not contribute to leveling up. They can still improve — it simply takes longer.
VARIANT RULE — PRACTICED GROWTH
This optional rule rewards characters who focus on training the skills that match their core Attributes.
When you Level Up, calculate how many of each Attribute's governed skills improved since your last level:
  0 governed skills improved  →  roll 1d4+1 (minimum 2)
  1–2 governed skills improved  →  roll 2d4+2
  3–4 governed skills improved  →  roll 3d4+3
  5+ governed skills improved  →  roll 5d4+5
You may still only choose 3 Attributes to increase per level up.
EXAMPLE: Oryn is a warrior with Long Blade (STR/AGI), Heavy Armor (END/STR), Block (STR/AGI), and Athletics (SPD/END) as Major skills — all trained hard this level. His STR governed skills improved 3 times (Long Blade, Block, Axe). He applies a ×3 multiplier to STR: rolls 3d4+3, getting 9+3 = 12. STR 55 becomes 67. Meanwhile his INT governed 0 skills — he rolls 1d4+1 for it if chosen, getting a modest 3. This rewards playing your archetype and makes divergence from your build feel mechanically meaningful.
Chapter 4: Pre-Built Archetypes
Archetypes are fully assembled character packages designed to get you to the table in minutes. Each includes a spread of Attributes, chosen Major and Minor skills, and an archetype-specific bonus. They are organized into three broad categories — Martial, Stealth & Social, and Arcane — so you can quickly find a role that suits you.
If you are using an archetype, skip Steps 2 and 3 of character creation (Chapter 11). Your Attributes and skill tiers are already handled. You still choose your Race and Birthsign, calculate derived statistics, and select starting equipment.
ARCHETYPES ARE STARTING POINTS
Nothing prevents a Knight from picking up Alchemy, or a Nightblade from wearing plate. Archetypes set where you begin, not where you end.
Martial Archetypes
Warriors, soldiers, and those for whom the answer to most problems is a weapon held in the right hand. Martial archetypes differ primarily in how they hit, how long they last, and how many things they can hit at once.
THE KNIGHT
Two-Handed Devastation
PLAYSTYLE
When a Knight enters a room, the room gets smaller. Greatswords, war axes, two-handed hammers — these are their instruments, and the damage they deal is extraordinary. Every swing burns Fatigue, so Knights are at their best in short, decisive engagements: kick in the door, clear the room, find somewhere to breathe. They falter in extended attrition fights and they are not subtle — but when they are at full Fatigue with a greatsword in both hands, there is almost nothing that hits harder.
IN THE PARTY
Primary damage dealer. Ends fights before the rest of the group runs low on resources. Veteran Knights learn to pace themselves and trust their allies to cover their flanks.
ATTRIBUTES
STR 65  END 55  AGI 35  SPD 35 INT 25  WIL 25  PER 35  LCK 30
MAJOR SKILLS
Blunt Weapon, Axe, Long Blade, Heavy Armor, Athletics
MINOR SKILLS
Block, Medium Armor, Speechcraft, Acrobatics, Vitalism
ARCHETYPE BONUS
+5 to STR damage bonus calculations. Start with an Iron Greatsword and Iron Chainmail.
THE SHIELD-BROTHER
Sword, Shield, and Staying Power
PLAYSTYLE
The Shield-Brother is the wall that holds. One weapon, one shield, and absolute refusal to go down — this archetype is built around surviving punishment that would collapse anyone else. Their Block skill makes incoming damage unreliable for enemies; their Vitalism dabbling lets them patch themselves mid-fight. Less raw damage than the Knight, but vastly more durable. In a long dungeon, that trade-off often wins.
IN THE PARTY
The party's anchor. When things go wrong — and they will — the Shield-Brother holds ground while allies recover. They are also typically the one who talks to the city guard, because they look like they have survived worse.
ATTRIBUTES

MINOR SKILLS

ARCHETYPE BONUS
+1 AR when using a shield. Start with an Iron Longsword, Iron Shield, and Iron Chainmail.
THE DUELIST
Twin Blades, Twice the Trouble
PLAYSTYLE
Fast, precise, and dangerous at close range — the Duelist's power comes from speed rather than raw strength. Two weapons mean two attacks per round, trading the penalty for relentless pressure that few opponents can weather. Agility drives both skill checks and damage, and their movement options make them difficult to pin down. Their weakness is durability: light armor and no shield means every hit that lands hurts.
IN THE PARTY
Damage dealer with mobility. Thrives when choosing engagements; struggles when the enemy controls the space. Experienced Duelists communicate: 'Keep their attention, and I will bleed them out.'
ATTRIBUTES
STR 40  END 40  AGI 65  SPD 50 INT 25  WIL 25  PER 35  LCK 30
MAJOR SKILLS
Short Blade, Long Blade, Acrobatics, Light Armor, Athletics
MINOR SKILLS
Block, Sneak, Marksman, Unarmored, Speechcraft
ARCHETYPE BONUS
Second attack has no penalty, and feats that would reduce the penalty make your second attack easier. Start with two Iron Shortswords and Leather Armor.
THE RANGER
Distance, Patience, and a Steady Hand
PLAYSTYLE
Damage dealt from beyond reach is damage you never have to absorb. Rangers keep enemies at range with bows and crossbows, ending threats before they become problems. Their Sneak and Athletics make them exceptional scouts — they often know what is in a room before their party reaches the door. If cornered in melee, they manage with a short blade, but staying out of arm's reach is always the plan.
IN THE PARTY
The party's eyes and first source of damage. They call positions, fire from cover, and move through terrain that slows everyone else.
ATTRIBUTES
STR 35  END 45  AGI 65  SPD 45 INT 30  WIL 25  PER 25  LCK 35
MAJOR SKILLS
Marksman, Sneak, Light Armor, Athletics, Short Blade
MINOR SKILLS
Acrobatics, Long Blade, Alchemy, Block, Vitalism
ARCHETYPE BONUS
Can fire a bow twice per turn a set number of times each combat equal to their character level. Start with a Short Bow, 20 arrows, and Leather Armor.
THE CRUSADER
Armor, Blade, and Holy Purpose
PLAYSTYLE
The Crusader fights in heavy plate and carries both a weapon and a working knowledge of Vitalism magic. They are not as offensively powerful as a pure Knight, nor as magically capable as a dedicated Priest — but they can do both, in the same encounter, without stopping. Healing themselves mid-combat, resisting disease and poison, and outlasting opponents are the Crusader's defining gifts.
IN THE PARTY
The party member who never quite dies. Excellent at protecting allies by drawing fire, buying time, and keeping everyone functional when things go badly wrong.

Stealth & Social Archetypes
Not every problem requires a sword. These archetypes excel at getting places they should not be, learning things they should not know, and talking their way out of situations they probably should not have gotten into.
THE NIGHTBLADE
Magic, Shadows, and a Knife
PLAYSTYLE
A scout wreathed in illusions moves through an occupied fortress, marking patrol routes in her mind. An assassin steps out of invisibility behind a mage, drives a dagger in, and vanishes before the body hits the floor. The Nightblade merges arcane capability with lethal precision — they use Illusion magic to get into position, then make the most of it.
IN THE PARTY
Shines where preparation meets opportunity. Requires more planning than a Knight, but a well-positioned Nightblade can eliminate a target before the rest of the party draws steel.
ATTRIBUTES

+1d6 damage from undetected position (once per combat unless re-hidden). Start with an Iron Dagger, Leather Armor, and two Apprentice Illusion spells.
THE ROGUE
Locks, Pockets, and Getting Away With It
PLAYSTYLE
A pickpocket relieves a wealthy merchant of his purse before the crowd's applause for a street performer fades. A burglar scales a garden wall and vanishes into a manor. A con artist gets a city official to sign something he did not mean to. Rogues master the non-combat space — they open doors that should not open, acquire things not for sale, and gather information meant to stay buried.
IN THE PARTY
Invaluable where raw force is wrong. Security and Speechcraft together mean most obstacles can be circumvented rather than confronted. In combat, a well-placed opening strike from hiding can change a fight's calculus entirely.

THE BARD
Words Are the Sharpest Instrument
PLAYSTYLE
A traveling performer finishes a ballad in a crowded tavern, then quietly extracts three secrets, a business proposition, and a map from the people who bought her drinks. A court herald speaks exactly the right words to defuse a situation thirty seconds from bloodshed. Bards are the game's ultimate social characters — they move through every level of society with ease, turning conversation into leverage and performance into access.
IN THE PARTY
Most valuable in campaigns with significant social and political content. Their broad knowledge makes them the party's best source of 'wait, I know something about this.'

THE SPY
Information Is the Only Currency That Matters
PLAYSTYLE
Every faction wants something, and the Spy makes it their business to know what. They work at the intersection of illusory magic and high-society Speechcraft — not skulking through shadows like a thief, but moving openly through the world with a different face and a perfected backstory. They are the archetype for players who want the game to reward careful preparation, patience, and the slow accumulation of exactly the right leverage.
IN THE PARTY
At their best when the party needs to infiltrate something rather than attack it. Their magic keeps them protected; their social skills keep them functional once inside. Requires a GM willing to run complex social environments with real stakes.

THE FENCE
Everything Has a Price. Knowing It Is the Skill.
PLAYSTYLE
The Fence is not primarily a thief, an assassin, or a con artist — they are a broker. They know who buys unusual things and who wants unusual things done. Their Mercantile skill is exceptional, their Speechcraft is precise, and their Security skill exists because the line between knowing where treasure is and acquiring it is thinner than most people acknowledge.
IN THE PARTY
Shines in campaigns where the party needs money, information, or introductions they cannot get by asking nicely. A skilled Fence can transform random dungeon finds into faction connections and maps nobody else has.

Arcane Archetypes
Arcane archetypes trade physical resilience and martial capability for access to the most powerful and versatile tools in the game. What they share is reliance on Magicka and the need for careful resource management.
THE BATTLE-MAGE
Steel and Spellfire, Walking Forward
PLAYSTYLE
A soldier in chainmail channels lightning through her sword as she closes on a formation. A combat veteran wades into melee, using magic as a force multiplier rather than a primary offense. The Battle-Mage refuses the mage's traditional fragility — they wear real armor, carry a real weapon, and managing both Magicka and Fatigue simultaneously is the central challenge and satisfaction of this archetype.
IN THE PARTY
A self-sufficient frontliner who brings options pure warriors lack. Opens with spellfire, presses with steel, finishes with whatever is necessary. Slower to master than either pure warriors or pure mages, but extraordinarily difficult to counter.
ATTRIBUTES
STR 45  END 45  AGI 35  SPD 30 INT 55  WIL 45  PER 25  LCK 30
MAJOR SKILLS
Elementalism, Long Blade, Medium Armor, Alteration, Block
MINOR SKILLS
Vitalism, Conjuration, Athletics, Heavy Armor, Divination
ARCHETYPE BONUS
+1 MP recovered on short rest. Start with an Iron Longsword, Iron Chainmail, and three Apprentice Elementalism spells.
THE SORCERER
Pure Power, Pure Consequence
PLAYSTYLE
An Adept Fireball turns a bandit ambush into a memory. A chain of lightning jumps from mercenary to mercenary. At full Magicka, the Sorcerer is the most offensively powerful character in any encounter. The cost is everything else: no armor, marginal melee ability, and an MP pool that empties fast at higher ranks. Good positioning is not an option — it is a survival requirement.
IN THE PARTY
Glass cannon in the most direct sense. When protected, they end fights. When exposed, they collapse. Playing one well means constant communication with your party.

ARCHETYPE BONUS
+2 bonus MP. Elementalism spells cost 1 less MP (min 1). Start with an Ironwood Staff and three Apprentice spells.
THE PRIEST
Life Before Everything
PLAYSTYLE
A battlefield healer moves between the wounded, never stopping. A temple scholar lays hands on the fevered and watches sickness retreat. The Priest keeps people alive — not glamorously, not violently, but reliably. Their Conjuration lets them call allies when direct action is unavoidable, and their Alchemy supplements healing with prepared potions and curative draughts.
IN THE PARTY
A Priest's contribution is measured in the distance between 'party survives this dungeon' and 'party does not.' Priests anchor many campaigns' moral tensions — questions about life, death, and obligation land hardest on the one who can heal.

ARCHETYPE BONUS
Vitalism spells cost 1 less MP (min 1). Potions brewed by you that restore HP, MP, or FP also restore +2 HP. Start with an Ironwood Staff and three Apprentice Vitalism spells.
THE SUMMONER
Never Fight Alone
PLAYSTYLE
A mage at the back of a room gestures, and something answers from beyond the veil. A bound blade materializes in an outstretched hand. A creature of smoke and fury stands between its summoner and an axe. Summoners fight through proxies — called entities and bound weapons that take damage the summoner does not. At full resources they can double a party's effective combat presence. Without Magicka, they are nearly helpless.
IN THE PARTY
Rewards patience and preparation. Not a front-line combatant; a force multiplier who keeps the actual combatants alive by providing things for enemies to fight instead.

THE ILLUSIONIST
The Fight That Never Happened
PLAYSTYLE
An entire patrol of guards passes within three feet of a motionless party, none of them seeing a thing. A fearsome beast turns on its handler, driven to frenzy by a whispered working. Illusionists win by preventing the fight from occurring at all, or by so thoroughly disrupting an enemy's ability to function that the fight is over before it starts.
IN THE PARTY
The most unconventional arcane archetype — and the one with the broadest applicability outside combat. They rarely deal damage. They often decide outcomes.

THE WITCH
Preparation Is the Greatest Weapon
PLAYSTYLE
Two dozen vials, carefully labeled, fill a satchel. Each was brewed over the last three days and will accomplish something a sword simply cannot. A poison slips into a target's wine. IN THE PARTY
A hybrid utility character with enormous depth. Thrown potions and poisons give real offensive capability that bypasses armor. Magic schools fill the gaps. Witches tend to be underestimated until someone is suddenly paralyzed.

THE ALCHEMIST
Every Problem Has a Formula
PLAYSTYLE
A traveling apothecary moves through markets buying mundane herbs that everyone else overlooks, knowing exactly what combinations they conceal. IN THE PARTY
Their greatest asset is preparation and self-sufficiency. Identifies every ingredient encountered, always knows what the party needs before a dungeon, fills gaps with bottled solutions. Thrown potions make them a real offensive threat when needed.

Chapter 4b: Birthsigns
Every character in Scrolls & Steel was born under one of the thirteen constellations recognized by Aethermoor's astronomers and mystics. Birthsigns are chosen at character creation and provide a permanent boon that tilts a character toward a particular style of play.
Birthsigns are roughly equal in power — they are not ranks, and no sign is objectively better than another. Each leans toward a different approach: raw power, utility, resilience, magic, or fortune. Choose the sign that best represents who your character is, or roll 1d13 if you prefer to let fate decide.

Chapter 5: Advancement, Feats & Dungeon Exploration
Characters in Scrolls & Steel grow through doing. A warrior who practices her blade every fight will, given enough fights, be a better sword fighter. A mage who pushes the limits of his Elementalism school will eventually stop flinching at Expert-rank spells. There is no experience point tally and no separate leveling currency — the process of play is the advancement system.
Skill Advancement
After any encounter, scene, or challenge in which you made a meaningful use of a skill — success or failure — place a small tick mark (✓) next to that skill. 'Meaningful' means the roll mattered: not a trivial action, but not necessarily a dramatic success either. The guard you failed to sneak past counts. The guard who never saw you also counts. But stealth-rolling down an empty corridor does not. 
What you track is exposure to challenge. Growth comes from being tested, not from succeeding. Each skill may only accumulate one tick between long rests.
Advancing a Skill at Rest
Roll d100. If the result is higher than your current skill rating, the skill increases by 1d4. Write the new total.
If the result is equal to or lower than your rating, no improvement — you need harder tests to keep growing.
Erase the tick mark regardless of outcome.
A Miscellaneous skill requires TWO meaningful uses to earn a single tick. Same advancement procedure applies.
ONE TICK PER SKILL PER REST: A skill may only accumulate one tick between long rests, regardless of how many times it is used meaningfully. Additional meaningful uses after the first do not add more ticks — the skill has already been sufficiently tested and the body needs rest to absorb the growth. This encourages breadth of activity over grinding a single skill.
ADVANCEMENT EXAMPLE
Vera has Sneak 47. She sneaks past two guards successfully and fails on a third. She marks a tick.
On long rest she rolls d100 and gets 61 — higher than 47. She rolls 1d4 and gets 3. Sneak becomes 50.
Three months later, Sneak is 78. She rolls 55 — lower than 78. The challenges she faced were no longer difficult enough to teach her anything new. To keep growing, she needs to attempt things that genuinely test Sneak 78.
Leveling Up
A Level Up represents a significant milestone of personal growth — not just improved skills, but a fundamental sharpening of instinct, awareness, and capacity. You don't tick off experience points to reach it; you improve specific skills until the sum of that improvement crosses a threshold.
You track two separate Growth Tallies on your sheet: one for Major Skills and one for Minor Skills. Each time a Major Skill increases by any amount, mark it in the Major Tally. When the Major Tally reaches 10, you gain a Level. Every time the Minor Tally reaches 20, you gain a bonus Level. Both tallies reset to 0 after triggering.
What Happens at Level Up
Choose 3 Attributes to increase. Each increases by 1d4+1 (minimum 2). See the Practiced Growth variant rule in Chapter 3 for multiplier-based gains.
Maximum HP increases by +2, maximum FP increases by +1, and maximum MP increases by +1. This goes on top of your ‘base’ maximums provided by your attribute scores (see chapter 2)
You gain one Feat (see below). You may bank it and spend it later when you meet a prerequisite.
Every 5 levels, you gain a Boon (see the Boons section).
ATTRIBUTE CAPS & SKILL CEILINGS
Attributes may be raised to a maximum of 100 through advancement. No Attribute may exceed 75 at character creation (after racial bonuses).
No skill may exceed its governing Attribute ceiling.
Enchantments, potions, and certain abilities can push past these caps temporarily.
Feats
Feats are specialized techniques and trained mastery that represent a deeper layer of a character's development — things that cannot be acquired simply by grinding a skill, but emerge from a particular combination of experience and deliberate focus. You gain one Feat at character creation and one more at each Level Up. You may bank Feats and spend them when you are ready.
Each Feat has a Prerequisite — a minimum skill or Attribute rating required before it can be taken.
A NOTE ON FEAT FREQUENCY
Gaining one Feat per level is the default rule. Groups should treat this as a starting point:
  • Every level (default): Characters feel frequently rewarded and grow quickly.
  • Every 2 levels: Feat choices feel more weighty; works well for longer campaigns.
  • Every 3–4 levels: Feats are rare and significant; appropriate for gritty, slow-burn settings.
There is no objectively correct frequency. Adjust based on how fast your group levels and how much they enjoy the feat-selection moment.

Boons
Every 5 levels, a character gains a Boon — a more powerful and permanent enhancement that reflects not just trained skill but fundamental change. Boons require both a skill threshold and at least one qualifying Feat already taken, ensuring they are only accessible to characters who have invested deeply in a particular path.
A character may hold one Boon per category at a time. Gaining a second in the same category replaces the first unless the GM rules otherwise.

Chapter 6: Skill Checks & Combat
Everything in Scrolls & Steel that depends on chance resolves through a single mechanic: roll a d100 and try to stay under a number. That number is your skill. The mechanic never changes. The stakes do.
The Roll-Under System
To attempt anything with an uncertain outcome, roll a d100. If you roll equal to or under your effective skill rating, you succeed. If you roll higher, you fail.
Effective Skill = Skill + Luck Bonus (LCK ÷ 10, round down)
Difficulty and Roll Modifiers
Difficulty modifies your roll result — it does not change your skill. Adding to your roll makes success harder; subtracting makes it easier.

SKILL CHECK EXAMPLE
Vera has Sneak 47 and Luck 30. Her Luck Bonus is 3. Her effective Sneak is 50.
Normal difficulty: she rolls 38. 38 ≤ 50 — she succeeds.
Hard difficulty (+10): she rolls 38. Modified result: 38 + 10 = 48. 48 ≤ 50 — barely succeeds. But if she rolled 41: modified result = 51. 51 > 50 — she fails despite a seemingly solid roll.
Very Hard (+20): even a roll of 31 becomes 51, a failure despite feeling like a good number. This is why difficulty matters — it compresses the margin for error, not the skill itself.
Advantage and Disadvantage
When circumstances strongly favor you, roll d100 twice and use the lower result. When circumstances strongly work against you, roll twice and use the higher. Apply difficulty modifiers after choosing which die to keep.
Critical Successes and Critical Failures
Rolling extremely low indicates exceptional precision. Rolling extremely high indicates a spectacular failure. These thresholds are fixed and equal in probability — every character, regardless of skill, has the same baseline chance of a critical moment. Note: by default, critical rolls also apply to non-combat skill checks. Check with your DM. 
Critical Success: Roll 1 or 2 on the d100 (a 2% chance). This always succeeds, regardless of skill or difficulty modifiers. On an attack, deal maximum possible damage. On a spell, choose a bonus effect at random from the list in Chapter 7. On both, ignore AR. On any other check, achieve an exceptional outcome at the GM's discretion.
Critical Failure: Roll 98 or 99. This always fails, regardless of skill. On a weapon attack, something goes wrong — you drop your weapon, stumble and lose your next Block, or strike an unintended target. On a spell, the MP is still spent but virtually anything can happen: your fireball might heal the enemy instead of burning them, your frost bolt might reverse and chill you, your Calm spell might drive the target into a Frenzy, or it might just fizzle out entirely. On any other check, the outcome is worse than a simple failure.
A Note on Criticals: A critical success might end a boss fight much earlier than the DM planned. A critical failure at a pivotal moment might mean the loss of an NPC or player character. These are unexpected but inevitable events at every table, and with practice and cooperation, can be just as fun, if not more, as if they don't happen. Practice as a DM and as a player to weave these unexpected events into the overarching story and experience at your table; the whole will be better off for it. A critical failure has just as much storytelling and roleplaying potential as a critical success. 
WHY FIXED THRESHOLDS?
Fixed critical thresholds (1–2 and 98–99) mean every character — novice or master — faces the same 2% risk of catastrophic failure and the same 2% chance of a shining moment. A veteran who rolls 99 drops their sword as surely as a recruit. A novice archer rolling a 2 gets lucky and finds the weak spot by accident. 
This keeps critical moments dramatic and equalizing rather than purely a function of skill level. Certain feats and Birthsigns can expand the critical success range. These are rare exceptions, and not what players should expect as the baseline.
Combat
Combat is structured into rounds, each representing roughly 6–10 seconds of action. Every combatant takes one turn per round, in order from highest to lowest Initiative. The GM runs groups of identical enemies as a single unit for simplicity.
Initiative
At the start of combat, each character rolls d10 and adds their Initiative score (AGI ÷ 10, round down). Highest total goes first. Ties go to the higher Agility.
On Your Turn
Move: Travel up to your Movement Speed in meters. A Full Move (forgoing your Action) doubles this distance.
Action: Attack, cast a spell, use an item, attempt a skill check, take cover, or help an ally.
Moving and attacking in the same turn: your attack roll suffers +10 (harder to hit while moving). 
Some abilities might list “free action” or “bonus action”. A free action does not cost any time and can happen alongside the other events during your turn. Bonus actions are like secondary actions: you don't have nearly the same breadth of options to spend these on, but you don't have to waste a full action on minor things, offering you more flexibility. A level 1 character likely won't have any options that use a bonus action, but as you progress, your range of options will expand. 
Universal Actions 
THROW: As an Action, you may throw a weapon tagged THROWN (see Appendix A), a potion, poison, or any item you can reasonably hurl. Thrown weapons use Marksman skill and deal damage based on the weapon. Thrown potions and poisons use Marksman skill at Short range (10m) as described in Chapter 8. At the DM’s discretion, they could deal 1d4 damage if hitting something directly, and apply their effect over short range (2m). You may throw an item and move in the same turn; the +10 moving-and-attacking penalty does not apply to throws made before movement begins.
DASH: spend your action and double your effective movement speed. This includes buffs or debuffs from spells and potions. 
Movement Speeds
Base movement is derived from Speed: (SPD ÷ 2, rounded to nearest 5) meters per turn, minimum 10m. Races may modify this baseline. Player characters can compare these against their lineage’s basic speed; the higher of two is their movement.

Attacking
Declare your attack and roll d100 against the relevant weapon skill, adding your Luck Bonus. If the modified roll is equal to or under your effective skill for the attack, the attack connects.
On a hit, roll the weapon's damage dice and add any applicable damage bonus. Subtract the target's Armor Rating (AR). The remainder is HP lost. If the result is 0 or negative, the armor absorbed the blow — the target takes 0 HP damage. 
One-Handed Weapons and Shields
Wielding a medium (one-handed) weapon with a shield offers real defensive benefits — but it costs you offensively. When attacking with a one-handed weapon while carrying a shield, your attack roll suffers an additional +5 modifier (harder to hit). This represents the slightly reduced swing arc, the divided attention between offense and the shield position, and the different body mechanics involved.
Alternatively, wielding a one-handed weapon in both hands (no shield) grants no penalty. You lose the shield's AR bonus and Block option, but attack unimpeded.
SUMMARY: ONE-HANDED WEAPON COMBAT OPTIONS
One-handed weapon + shield:  +5 to attack rolls (harder). +1 AR from shield. Can Block.
One-handed weapon two-handed: no attack penalty. No AR bonus. Cannot Block.
One-handed weapon + off-hand weapon: see Dual Wielding rules.

It doesn’t matter how many years of experience you have; if you’re too tired to lift your blade, you’re in trouble.

Blocking
Blocking is declared as a Reaction at the start of an incoming attack — before the attacker rolls to hit. This represents raising your shield or bracing your weapon before you know whether the blow will connect.
When you declare a Block:
1. Declare the Block before the attacker rolls.
2. The attacker rolls their attack as normal.
3. If the attack hits, roll your Block skill. On a success, halve the incoming damage after AR. On a failure, full damage lands.
4. If the attack misses on its own (the attacker’s roll fails), your Block declaration is wasted for that attack and costs your Reaction.
Requires a shield or two-handed weapon parry. One Block Reaction allowed per round.
Dual Wielding
A character wielding two Light or Medium weapons may declare a Dual Strike on their turn — a normal attack with the primary hand, then a second attack with the off-hand at +20 to roll that doesn’t apply the STR or AGI damage bonus that weapon would usually get. Heavy weapons always require two hands, and cannot Dual Strike.
FP cost for Dual Strike: pay the FP cost for each weapon separately (e.g., two light weapons cost 1+1 = 2 FP for the attacks, plus the passive 1 FP per round). The two attacks are made in the same action.

Engagement and Disengaging
When two creatures are in active melee (they have attacked or been attacked by each other this round), they are considered Engaged. Leaving an Engaged enemy’s melee reach without taking a Disengage action provokes a free Reaction strike.
DISENGAGE ACTION: Spend your full Action to safely disengage. You may move normally this turn without provoking Reaction strikes from any Engaged enemy.
REACTION STRIKE: When a creature leaves your melee reach without disengaging, you may make one free attack against them at +10 difficulty (harder to hit a moving target). This uses your Reaction for the round. You may only make one Reaction strike per round total.
Note: Creatures that use a Full Move (double movement) are not automatically provoking — they are sprinting. Only creatures who move past or away from you at normal movement speed while in melee contact trigger this. The GM has final say in ambiguous cases.
Grappling
To grapple a creature, declare a Grapple instead of making a normal attack. Both parties make opposed rolls: the attacker rolls Athletics, the defender rolls Athletics or Acrobatics (their choice). The attacker wins ties.
GRAPPLED condition: The grappled creature cannot move from its current position. All its attack rolls are at Disadvantage. Casting any spell requires a WIL check at +20 first — failure means the spell does not fire and MP is not spent.
MAINTAINING A GRAPPLE: The grappler must spend their Action each round to maintain the hold. They may not make any attacks other than Fistfight while maintaining. They may move at half speed, dragging the grappled creature with them.
BREAKING FREE: The grappled creature may spend their Action to make another opposed Athletics roll to escape. Success ends the Grappled condition.
GRAPPLE SIZE LIMIT: You cannot grapple a creature more than one size category larger than yourself. A human cannot grapple a Cave Troll.

Dungeon Exploration & The Crawl
A dungeon — or any dangerous, enclosed space under enemy control — operates by different rules from the open road. Resources do not recover easily. Enemies become more alert the longer you linger. The tension is a feature, not a problem to solve.
The Alert Level
At the start of any dungeon or enemy-controlled area, the GM sets the Alert Level to 0. As the party moves through the space, Alert Level increases. It represents the collective awareness of the dungeon's occupants — not every enemy knows exactly where you are, but the population as a whole is becoming more vigilant, more active, and less predictable.

Wandering Encounters
A wandering encounter is an unscheduled enemy or hazard the party stumbles into — or that stumbles into them — as a result of the dungeon's population reacting to their presence. They are distinct from scripted encounters (guarding a specific room or treasure) in that they happen between locations, during rests, or in response to noise.
The GM rolls for wandering encounters on a d10 whenever the party:
Takes a short rest at Alert 6 or higher.
Moves slowly or spends more than 10 minutes in an area without progressing.
Makes significant noise (failed Sneak check in a patrol route, loud combat, triggered trap).
Has triggered any event that raised Alert by 2 or more in the last 10 minutes.

Encounter Composition
The GM populates wandering encounters based on the dungeon's known population. In a bandit hideout, wandering encounters are bandits — stragglers, messengers, off-duty guards. In an undead ruin, they are more likely to be animated revenants and zombies, In a cave system, they might be entirely unaffiliated creatures sharing the space with the dungeon's main faction.
A useful shorthand: wandering encounters should feel like interruptions, not set-pieces. They rarely have specific treasure or information. Their purpose is to drain resources, raise stakes, and keep the party moving.
Traps
Dungeons, ruins, and secured vaults are often protected by mechanical traps — pressure plates, tripwires, falling ceilings, dart launchers, poison needles, and worse. The Security skill governs all trap-related checks.
DETECTING TRAPS: A character with the Trap Sense feat may actively search for traps as an Action. Those without the feat may still notice obvious signs (a suspiciously clean stone, a thin wire at ankle height) by declaring they’re searching and succeeding on an Acrobatics or Perception-adjacent Divination check (Very Hard, +20). The GM may also offer clues proactively when characters move slowly and cautiously.
DISARMING TRAPS: Characters with the Careful Hands feat may attempt to disarm a detected trap. Roll Security (Normal difficulty for simple traps, Hard for complex, Very Hard for masterwork). Success disarms the trap safely. Failure does not trigger the trap but makes the next attempt Hard (+10 cumulative). Critical failure triggers the trap.
TRIGGERING A TRAP: When a trap activates, all creatures in its area of effect must make the saving throw listed in the trap’s description (typically an END or AGI roll at Normal or Hard difficulty). Trap effects use the same magnitude tiers as spells and alchemy.
SAMPLE TRAPS
DART TRAP: A pressure plate fires 1d4 poisoned darts at the triggering creature. Each dart: Marksman 50 to hit vs target AR. Hit: 1d4 Piercing + Damage Health Low (1 HP/round for 3 rounds, END roll Normal to resist poison).
PITFALL: A disguised trapdoor opens beneath a creature. AGI roll (Normal) to react. Failure: fall 3m (1d6 Crushing, AR does not apply). Alert +2.
ALARM WIRE: A near-invisible wire triggers a loud bell or horn. No damage. Alert +4 immediately. Very Easy (−20) to spot if moving carefully.
SWINGING BLADE: A blade swings across a doorway at chest height. AGI roll (Hard) to duck. Failure: 2d6 Slashing. Success: no damage.
POISON GAS VIAL: A pressure plate shatters a sealed vial. All creatures within 3m make END roll (Normal). Failure: Poisoned (Damage Health Medium, 2 HP/round for 3 rounds).
FALLING CEILING: A wide trigger plate causes a section of ceiling to descend quickly. All creatures in a 3m zone: AGI roll (Hard). Failure: 3d6 Crushing. Success: half damage. Alert +3.
Resting in a Dungeon
Short rests (10 minutes, half max FP, WIL÷10 MP recovered) can be taken at Alert 0–5 without additional risk. At Alert 6+, the party must secure or barricade a space before resting — failure to do so results in an automatic wandering encounter roll.
Long rests (8 hours, all resources recovered, skill advancement permitted) are nearly impossible inside a dungeon above Alert 4. The party should expect to complete the dungeon without a full rest, or to retreat, rest outside, and return.
RE-ENTERING A DUNGEON AFTER LEAVING TO REST
When the party exits a dungeon to take a long rest and then returns, the dungeon does not forget.
Each time the party re-enters after a rest:
  First re-entry:  Alert resets to 2. The dungeon is on edge but not yet mobilized.
  Second re-entry:  Alert starts at 4. Patrols have reorganized.
  Third re-entry:  Alert starts at 6. Active search protocols.
  Fourth re-entry:  Alert starts at 8. They know you are coming.
Additionally, enemies in previously cleared areas have partially repopulated — roughly half the original occupants have been replaced, reinforced, or replaced by something worse. The dungeon adapts.
GM GUIDANCE — THE CRAWL IS A CLOCK
Think of Alert Level as a slow countdown. Every decision the players make either costs time (raises Alert) or doesn't. The crawl creates pacing without requiring a literal ticking bomb — the players understand they cannot linger, even when rules don't explicitly forbid it.
Not every dungeon needs this system. A clearly abandoned ruin or small bandit hideout may not warrant Alert tracking. Reserve it for truly hostile, occupied, or time-sensitive spaces.

Magic in Scrolls & Steel is not a gift or a birthright. It is a discipline, cultivated through study, practice, and the willingness to push past what should be possible. Every mage carries a pool of Magicka drawn from their own Intelligence and honed by their Willpower — it empties and recovers like breath, like strength, like every other resource that makes survival possible.
Magicka Points (MP)
Your Magicka Pool equals INT ÷ 5 (round down). Spending MP to cast spells draws from this pool. Running out means no more magic until you rest.

Casting a Spell
1. Check you have enough MP. If not, you cannot cast.
2. Spend the MP cost.
3. Spend 1 FP.
4. Roll d100 against the relevant magic school skill (add Luck Bonus).
5. On success, the spell works. On failure, MP and FP are spent — the energy dissipated without effect. Even on a failure, the attempt counts as a meaningful use of that magic school and earns a tick toward skill advancement.
Critical Casting Results
Critical Success (natural 1 or 2): choose one bonus effect:
Double the spell's duration.
Extend the spell's range by one step (Short → Long → Very Long → Extreme).
Increase the spell's magnitude by one tier (Low → Medium → High → Extreme).
The spell costs half its normal MP (round up) — the surplus flows back into your reserves.
Critical Failure (natural 98 or 99): the spell misfires. MP and FP are still lost. The GM determines the effect — often disorienting rather than catastrophic, but occasionally worse.

THE GUIDING QUESTION
When determining magnitude for a custom spell: 'What would this effect cost if I bought it as a standard spell?'
If the answer is Apprentice, it's Low. Journeyman = Medium. Adept = High. Expert/Master = Extreme.
The standard spell list is your calibration tool — every custom spell should feel like it belongs in that list.
Spellcrafting
The standard spell list is a foundation. Spellcrafting is where the system opens. Characters who have learned Effect Components can assemble entirely custom spells — combining effects, adjusting parameters, and creating magic that no scrollmaker has ever written down.
You can only craft spells using Effects you have already learned. Reading a Firebolt scroll teaches you the Fire Damage effect. Reading a Calm scroll teaches you the Calm effect. These are the building blocks. You can find the full list of spell effects in Appendix E.
Spellcrafting is both a gentle process and a taxing endeavor. Some spell effects will mesh weirdly, or outright contradict each other. It doesn’t quite make sense for a firebolt to also heal the target, but you can certainly make a spell that does so. Work with your DM when crafting a new spell; two heads are better than one when it comes to deciphering the arcane.

SPELLCRAFTING EXAMPLE — 'EMBER VEIL'
Daevinne has learned Fire Damage (from Firebolt) and Silence (from a scroll).
She designs: high fire damage on touch, plus a brief Silence on the same target.

She names it 'Ember Veil.' Since it draws on Elementalism and Illusion, her spellcrafting roll uses:

VARIANT RULE — UNSTABLE SUMMONING
Standard Conjuration spells summon creatures that are reliably allied. This variant introduces two alternate summoning modes that trade cost for reliability.
CONTROLLED SUMMON (+1 MP above base cost): Spend 1 extra MP when casting any summoning spell. The creature arrives fully cooperative, does not leave until dismissed, and will follow complex instructions. Use this when you need certainty.
UNSTABLE SUMMON (reduce cost by 1–5 MP): You may reduce the MP cost of a summoning spell by up to 5. When you cast it, roll 1d6 after the spell resolves to determine the creature’s attitude. The more you reduce the cost, the wider the range of bad outcomes.
INSTABILITY TABLE: The d6 result determines the outcome based on how many MP you reduced the cost by:
Reduced by 1: Roll 2–6 = summons normally, hostile to your enemies. Roll 1 = creature is confused and leaves immediately after combat.
Reduced by 2: Roll 4–6 = summons normally. Roll 2–3 = creature leaves after combat (not hostile to you, but won’t fight). Roll 1 = creature is hostile to everything.
Reduced by 3: Roll 5–6 = summons normally. Roll 3–4 = hostile to everything, including party members. Roll 1–2 = creature is allied with your enemies and hostile to your party.
Reduced by 4: Roll 6 = summons normally. Roll 4–5 = leaves after combat. Roll 2–3 = hostile to everything. Roll 1 = allied with enemies, specifically targets the summoner first.
Reduced by 5: Roll 6 = summons normally. Roll 5 = leaves after combat. Roll 3–4 = hostile to everything. Roll 1–2 = allied with enemies and hostile to party, prioritizing the summoner.
A spell cannot be reduced below 1 MP. If the instability roll produces a hostile result, the summoner cannot attempt to dismiss or regain control of the summon during that combat.
Concentration
Some spells require active focus to maintain — they are Concentration spells. A spell with a duration of 1 minute or longer that produces an ongoing effect (not an instant damage spell) is typically a Concentration spell. Standard examples: Invisibility, Silence, Frenzy, Slow, Shield, Detect Life.
Once the duration of a spell using concentration is up, the caster may make a WIL check to continue casting the spell. Each round that they continue casting the spell past its duration, that check gets +10 to the roll (harder) and they must expend 1 MP.
A caster may maintain only one Concentration spell at a time. Casting a second Concentration spell immediately ends the first.
CONCENTRATION BROKEN BY DAMAGE: If a caster maintaining a Concentration spell takes any HP damage, they must make a WIL check (Normal). On failure, the spell ends immediately. If the caster is Knocked Down or Grappled, they must also make this check.
The False Presence feat (Illusion 60) extends Concentration spells by one additional round before the WIL check is triggered. The Wandering Eye birthsign’s −5 penalty to Concentration checks applies here.
Extended Casting: Spells That Take Multiple Rounds
Some high-powered custom spells — or standard Expert and Master spells in certain variants — can be designated as Extended Cast spells. An Extended Cast spell takes 2 or more full rounds to complete. During this time:
• The caster may not move or make attacks.
• If the caster takes any damage, they must make a WIL check (Hard, +10). Failure wastes the casting: MP is not spent, but FP costs for those rounds are.
• On completion, the spell fires and is resolved normally.
The benefit of Extended Casting is a cost reduction: a 2-round cast reduces MP cost by 1 (minimum 1), a 3-round cast reduces by 2, etc. The Ritual Caster boon already covers the extreme version of this (10-minute ritual = Expert spell, no MP).
The Spellcrafting Skill Check
Spellcrafting Roll = (Enchanting + all contributing school skills) ÷ (number of skills), rounded down
Example: A spell using Elementalism and Illusion. Enchanting 50, Elementalism 65, Illusion 55. (50+65+55) ÷ 3 = 56. Roll against 56 + Luck Bonus.
Two Ways to Craft a Spell
Option 1: Craft It Yourself
Requires: blank spell scroll and ink (~30 gold), uninterrupted time (30 minutes per MP cost of final spell), and the MP the spell itself will require.
Roll the Spellcrafting skill check at the end.
Success: the spell is inscribed. Failure: materials consumed, must restart with new materials.
You must know all Effect Components used.
Option 2: Commission a Mage
Find a specializing mage (Mages Guild Spellwrights usually available).
Cost: roughly 100 gold per MP cost of the final spell. Mages that specialize in that school of magic might charge less as it will be easier for them; less practiced mages might charge more. 
No roll required. Spell ready the following morning.
You must describe the desired effect and teach any unknown effects.

Cost: roughly 125 gold per MP cost of the effect + soul gem (your own or purchased at markup).
No roll required. Work completes the next day.
Chapter 8: Alchemy
Alchemy is the art of reading what the natural world contains and persuading it to become something useful. Where magic requires Magicka and enchanting requires soul gems, alchemy requires patience and knowledge — the willingness to pick up what others walk past and understand what it becomes when combined with something else.
Unlike spells, potions are physical objects. Anyone can drink one. Anyone can throw one. This democratization of magical effect is alchemy's great strength, and it is why even non-magical parties benefit enormously from having a practiced alchemist.
Brewing Potions
To brew, you need at least two ingredients sharing at least one property, and access to apparatus. The process takes approximately 10 minutes.
Roll d100 against your Alchemy skill.
Success: produce a potion with the shared property. Ingredients consumed.
Strong success (roll 20+ under your skill): two shared properties, or one property at the next higher magnitude.
Failure: ingredients ruined. Nothing useful produced.
When brewing, declare whether each potion is crafted as an INGESTIBLE or a THROWABLE before rolling. The base ingredients and Alchemy roll are identical for both — only the form differs.
INGESTIBLE: Drunk or applied directly to skin or wound. Full magnitude effect. Standard use.
THROWABLE: Sealed for shattering on impact. Effect applies in a 2m radius at the point of impact (Marksman roll to target a specific creature; area effects need no roll). Effect is one magnitude lower than an equivalent ingestible potion at the same cost. Example: a High-magnitude Restore Health ingestible restores 2d8 HP — the throwable version restores 1d8 (Medium). The Potionwright feat negates this reduction.
Alchemy Apparatus
Higher-quality tools raise the ceiling of what your skill can achieve — they do not add a flat bonus to your roll, but allow higher-magnitude results.
Apparatus
Effect
No apparatus (improvised)
Effective skill ceiling capped at 30 for this brew. Low magnitude only.

Discovering Properties
Taste-test: Eat a small sample. Reveals the ingredient's first property, and applies it to you very weakly (an ingredient that has paralysis as the first property might paralyze you for 5 seconds, one that fortified INT might make you smarter by 1 for 15 seconds, etc) 
Experimental brew: combine two unknowns. If they share a property, you produce something and can analyze it. Roll at +10 (harder).
Pay a scholar: herbalists and Guild mages can identify ingredients. Usually 5–20 gold each.
Poisons and Thrown Alchemicals
Applying a poison to a weapon takes a bonus action or 10 seconds out of combat. The poison delivers its effect on the next successful hit. By default, consumed on that hit.
Throwing a potion or poison is a ranged attack using Marksman skill (Short range, 10m; or Long range 30m with the Far Throw feat). A shattering throwable vial applies its effect in a 2m radius around the point of impact — all creatures in that radius are affected. Targeted poisons aimed at a specific creature require a Marksman roll to hit; splash effects affecting an area do not.
THROWN ALCHEMICALS IN PLAY
A thrown Paralyze poison shatters between two enemies — both must resist.
A thrown Restore Health potion reaches an ally 8m away who can't be reached safely.
A Fire Damage alchemical (Redglow + Ashvein) deals its effect as a splash on impact.
The Alchemical Arm feat: +1 magnitude on thrown items; prime and throw in one action.

Chapter 11: Character Creation
Building a character in Scrolls & Steel takes between 15 and 30 minutes, depending on how much you want to customize. Follow these steps in order. Steps 2 and 3 can be skipped if you use a pre-built archetype.
Step 1 — Choose a Race
Pick a race from Chapter 12. Races provide Attribute bonuses, skill bonuses, a racial ability, and a base movement speed.
Step 2 — Assign Attributes (Custom Characters)
Set all Attributes to 40 and distribute 30 additional points freely. No Attribute above 65 before racial bonuses; cap is 75 after racial bonuses at character creation. Attributes may be raised to 100 through leveling. Alternatively, distribute 350 points from scratch.
Step 3 — Choose Skills (Custom Characters)
Choose 5 Major Skills (start at 40) and 5 Minor Skills (start at 25). The rest are Miscellaneous (start at 5). Apply racial skill bonuses. Then, distribute 5 points among the Major Skills, 5 among the Minor Skills, and 5 among the Miscellaneous skills. 
Step 4 — Choose a Birthsign
Choose one of the thirteen Birthsigns from Chapter 4b. This is permanent.
Step 5 — Calculate Derived Statistics
HP = END ÷ 5 (round down)
MP = INT ÷ 5 (round down)
FP = (END + STR + AGI + SPD) ÷ 10 (round down)
Initiative = AGI ÷ 10 (round down)
Luck Bonus = LCK ÷ 10 (round down) — add to every roll, or use as Luck Dice (see Chapter 2 variant)
Melee Damage Bonus = STR ÷ 20 (round down)
Agility Damage Bonus = AGI ÷ 20 (round down)
Movement Speed = SPD ÷ 2 (round to nearest 5) meters per turn (check with lineage baseline speed; the higher of the two is your speed)
Step 6 — Starting Equipment
One weapon appropriate to a Major or Minor combat skill.
Armor appropriate to your primary armor skill, or simple clothing if none.
Pack: bedroll, 1 week rations, tinderbox, waterskin, 25 gold.
Spellcasting characters: two Apprentice spells in chosen school(s).
Step 7 — Gain Your First Feat
All characters receive one Feat at character creation. Choose any Feat for which you meet the prerequisite, or bank it for when you do. 
Step 8 — Background
Work with your GM to establish where you came from and why you are adventuring. Background is narrative — your skills already reflect your history mechanically.
Worked Examples
The following examples walk through character creation from start to finish. The first follows a player using a pre-built archetype; the second builds a character entirely from scratch.
EXAMPLE 1: USING A PRE-BUILT ARCHETYPE (ALDRIC THE KNIGHT)
Aldric’s player wants a heavily armored melee fighter. She chooses the Knight archetype from Chapter 4, which handles Steps 2 and 3 for her.
STEP 1 — RACE: She picks Nordal (the Frost-Kin). This gives +10 STR, +5 END, −5 INT, −5 WIL, −5 PER, +10 Heavy Armor, +5 Long Blade. Base movement 20m.
STEPS 2 & 3 — SKIPPED: The Knight archetype sets her Attributes to STR 55, END 50, AGI 35, SPD 35, INT 30, WIL 30, PER 30, LCK 30. After Nordal bonuses: STR 65, END 55, AGI 35, SPD 35, INT 25, WIL 25, PER 25, LCK 30. Major Skills (start at 40): Heavy Armor, Long Blade, Block, Athletics, Blunt Weapon. Minor Skills (start at 25): Security, Speechcraft, Vitalism, Armorer, Marksman. Distributing points for her skills, she raises Block by 5 (45), Armorer by 5 (30), and Enchanting by 5 (10).
STEP 4 — BIRTHSIGN: She chooses The Warrior’s Brand: +10 to STR or AGI at creation. She picks STR, bringing it to 75 (the cap after racial).
STEP 5 — DERIVED STATISTICS: HP = 55 ÷ 5 = 11. MP = 25 ÷ 5 = 5. FP = (55+75+35+35) ÷ 10 = 20. Initiative = 35 ÷ 10 = 3. Luck Bonus = 30 ÷ 10 = 3. Melee Damage Bonus = 75 ÷ 20 = 3. Movement = 20m.
STEP 6 — EQUIPMENT: Steel Longsword, Iron Plate Armor, Tower Shield, standard pack, 100 gold.
STEP 7 — FEAT: She takes Steady Footing (Block 40 prerequisite): she cannot be knocked Prone by physical attacks that deal less than 5 dmg after AR.
STEP 8 — BACKGROUND: Aldric is a disgraced knight whose lord was killed by a rival. She now works as a sell-sword, pursuing the man responsible. Her skills already reflect a military career.
EXAMPLE 2: BUILDING FROM SCRATCH (TESSARA THE WANDERING MAGE)
Tessara’s player wants a self-taught mage who also knows her way around a dagger. She builds entirely from scratch.
STEP 1 — RACE: She picks Vethanirum (the Borderborn). Bonuses: +5 INT, +5 WIL, +5 PER. Penalties: −5 STR, −5 END, −5 AGI. Racial ability: 25% magic resistance. +5 to all magic schools. Base movement 20m.
STEP 2 — ATTRIBUTES: Starting at 40 each, she distributes 30 extra points: +10 INT (50), +10 WIL (50), +5 AGI (45), +5 PER (45). After racial bonuses: STR 35, END 35, AGI 40, SPD 40, INT 55, WIL 55, PER 50, LCK 40.
STEP 3 — SKILLS: Major Skills (40): Elementalism, Illusion, Alteration, Short Blade, Sneak. Minor Skills (25): Vitalism, Conjuration, Speechcraft, Alchemy, Light Armor. Racial +5 to all magic schools applied: Elementalism 45, Illusion 45, Alteration 45, Vitalism 30, Conjuration 30. Finally, she distributes the final 5 points among each category of skills: for her major skills, she gives all 5 to Elementalism (raised to 50). Among her minor skills, she gives 3 to Conjuration (to 33) and 2 to Vitalism (32). For her Miscellaneous skills, she gives all 5 to Enchanting (10).
STEP 4 — BIRTHSIGN: She chooses The Scholar’s Lantern: +10 INT at creation (now 65) and faster Miscellaneous skill advancement.
STEP 5 — DERIVED STATISTICS: HP = 35 ÷ 5 = 7. MP = 65 ÷ 5 = 13. FP = (35+35+40+40) ÷ 10 = 15. Initiative = 40 ÷ 10 = 4. Luck Bonus = 40 ÷ 10 = 4. Agility Damage Bonus = 40 ÷ 20 = 2. Movement = 20m.
STEP 6 — EQUIPMENT: Steel Dagger, plain clothing (no armor skill used), standard pack, two Apprentice Elementalism spells (Fire Bolt and Frost Touch), 25 gold.
STEP 7 — FEAT: She takes Focused Casting (Elementalism 50): Elementalism spells cost 1 less MP (minimum 1). Her Fire Bolt now costs 1 MP instead of 2.
STEP 8 — BACKGROUND: Tessara grew up in a merchant family and taught herself magic from stolen textbooks. She is looking for a mentor — and possibly revenge on the guild that rejected her application.
Resting & Downtime
Characters in Scrolls & Steel recover HP, MP, and FP through rest. How much they recover depends on the quality and length of the rest, and on whether they are safe enough to truly sleep.
Catching Your Breath
If you don’t have time for even a short rest, simply catching your breath can still help you refocus. For each minute of rest (very light activity - DM’s discretion), regain 1 FP.
Short Rest
A short rest is approximately 10 minutes of sitting, bandaging wounds, and other very light activity. You must not be in immediate danger or inside a dungeon at Alert 6 or higher.
On a short rest, each character recovers: half of their maximum FP (round down), 1d4 MP, and 1d4 HP. You may take up to two short rests between long rests. A third short rest in the same day provides no benefit.
Long Rest
A long rest is a full night’s sleep or equivalent — at least 8 hours in a safe location. Sleeping in a dungeon at Alert 4 or higher is dangerous (see Chapter 6), and a long rest may trigger a wandering encounter instead.
On a long rest, each character recovers: all HP, all MP, all FP, and all limited-use abilities that refresh on a long rest.
If the party is in an inn or similar comfortable location, they also clear all Fatigue conditions accumulated during the day. When camping in the wild, they only remove one. 
Downtime Activities
Between adventures, when the party has days or weeks of unstructured time, characters may undertake Downtime Activities. The GM sets how many days are available. Each activity takes at least one full day.
TRAIN A SKILL. Spend 3 days practicing a skill under a tutor or through self-directed study. Roll d100 vs. your current skill at the end: on a success (roll equal to or under), you gain +1d4 to that skill. Tutors cost 10–50 gold per day depending on their expertise.
CRAFTING. Spend 1 day per item crafting potions (Alchemy), enchanted items (Enchanting), or repairing equipment (Armorer). You must have the appropriate materials and tools. Roll against the relevant skill when the work is done.
GATHER INFORMATION. Spend 1–3 days in a settlement asking questions. Make a Speechcraft or Mercantile roll. On a success, the GM provides one useful piece of information about a target (a person, location, organization, or rumor). On a strong success (rolling 20+ under your skill), you gain two pieces.
ESTABLISH CONNECTIONS. Spend 3 days actively building a relationship with a faction, guild, or individual. Make a Speechcraft roll. On a success, your Disposition with that group or person increases by 5. Connections open doors to jobs, discounts, and information. This activity cannot be repeated with the same target until significant time has passed.
RECOVER FROM INJURY. If a character suffered a severe injury (broken bone, lasting wound — GM discretion), rest alone is not enough. Spending 3–7 days resting under a healer’s care, or a Vitalism roll by a trained character each day, clears the condition. Attempting an adventure before recovery risks worsening the injury.
CAROUSING. Spend 1 day and at least 10 gold celebrating. Roll d100: on a 01–20, something memorable (and possibly problematic) happened — the GM rolls or picks an event. On a 21–80, nothing eventful occurred. On an 81–100, you made a useful contact or had a genuinely good time: +1 to Luck for the next adventure (does not stack). Carousing is optional, but it is a good way for the GM to introduce rumors, complications, and local color.
Chapter 12: Peoples of Aethermoor
Twelve peoples inhabit the known world, grouped into five categories: Elven peoples (sharing the suffix -irum), Dwarven peoples (sharing the prefix Apis-), Human peoples, Orc peoples, and Beastborn peoples. At character creation, choose a specific Lineage — not just a broad category. Most mechanical traits are tied to lineage.
All lineages provide a net +15 bonus and −15 penalty to Attributes, plus skill bonuses and a racial ability. No lineage is superior to another — they simply excel at different things.
I. The Elven Peoples — the -irum
All elven peoples share the suffix -irum — an ancient grammatical marker translating loosely as 'who endure.' Elves are among the longest-lived peoples of Aethermoor; plenty outlive entire human kingdoms. Four elven lineages exist in meaningful numbers, each having diverged so dramatically in culture, physiology, and magical attunement that the shared suffix can feel like a polite fiction — though most elves would hotly dispute this.
Lineage: Solarirum — the Gilt-Born
The Solarirum trace their lineage to the oldest elven civilization, a culture of scholar-casters that shaped the continent's earliest institutions. Tall, angular, and faintly luminescent in direct sunlight, most live in layered city-states where magical study is as ordinary as farming is elsewhere. They regard themselves as custodians of knowledge — a belief their neighbors find either admirable or insufferable, depending on how recently they've had to deal with one.

II. The Dwarven Peoples — the Apis-
All dwarven peoples share the prefix Apis-, from an old root in their own tongue meaning 'of the stone.' Dwarves are stocky, dense, and slower than most races — built for endurance and strength, not speed. Their base movement is 15m regardless of SPD derivation. This is a genuine trade-off, compensated by exceptional resilience and the Stonestep feat available to all dwarven lineages.
STONESTEP FEAT (Dwarven Lineages Only — No Other Prerequisite)
Your movement never triggers opportunity-style reactions from enemies.
You can move through occupied spaces (squeezing past enemies in corridors).
You cannot be involuntarily moved — shoved, blown back, grapple-dragged — unless the source is magical.
This feat may be taken at character creation as your free starting feat.

Stocky even by dwarven standards, with a well-earned reputation for stubbornness and craftsmanship that they consider deeply accurate and not remotely an insult. Their ales are the best in the known world, which they will tell you unprompted.

Lineage: Apisveldir — the Gem Seekers
The Apisveldir are the rarest and most widely misunderstood of the dwarven peoples. To outsiders their fixation on rare gemstones looks like greed — they will divert an entire expedition to investigate a glimmer in a far tunnel, spend a month's wages on a single flawless sapphire, and turn down gold in favour of a fragment of something rarer. To the Apisveldir themselves, this is not acquisitiveness. It is closer to recognition.
Apisveldir believe that exceptional gemstones are not merely beautiful — they are the physical memory of geological time, compressed emotion, and in some cases echoes of souls too ancient to be contained by mortal vessels. Their theology holds that certain gems are listening. Most non-Apisveldir find this unsettling. The Apisveldir consider that a limitation of imagination.

Lineage: Kreln — the Deepstone Kin
The Kreln are the remnants of a dwarven civilization that, long before recorded history, retreated entirely into the deep ocean. No one knows whether it was catastrophe, choice, or something stranger that drove them below the surface. What remained emerged over millennia as something distinctly its own: stocky and dense like their landbound cousins, but shaped by crushing pressure and lightless depths into a form that unsettles those encountering them for the first time.
Kreln bodies are compact and low-slung, built to withstand forces that would rupture other creatures. Their skin ranges from deep blue-grey to mottled greenish-black, with faint bioluminescent patches along the jaw, collarbones, and the backs of the hands — dim, cool light that pulses slowly when they are calm and brightens under stress. Many have partial carapace growth: ridged, calcified plates of bone-like material along the shoulders, shins, and the crown of the skull, their edges irregular and barnacle-roughed. Their eyes are enormous and pale, adapted for deep water — on the surface, many squint against bright light and wear smoked glass lenses carved from obsidian.
The Kreln do not have a unified civilization above water. Small enclaves settle in coastal caves, flooded ruins, and port-city underbellies. Most who travel the wider world do so because their settlement was destroyed, because they were exiled, or because they are searching for something their people lost long ago.

III. The Human Peoples
Humans are the most numerous and culturally varied peoples of Aethermoor. They lack the extreme longevity of elves and dwarves, which drives an urgency and ambition that other peoples sometimes find exhausting and occasionally admirable.

IV. The Orc Peoples
The orcish peoples of Aethermoor are as varied as any other group — they have their own internal politics, theological disagreements, and complex views on who counts as kin. Two lineages are described here. The Gorirum claim ancient kinship with the elven peoples, a position their formal name advertises directly with the -irum suffix and which generates significant controversy in elven circles.
Lineage: Stoneguard — the Iron-Blooded
Stoneguard culture is organized around the war-band, the smith, and the concept of earned authority. They are direct, pragmatic, and deeply invested in the idea that capability matters more than birth. Their settlements are fortified and self-sufficient — built for war not because they seek it, but because they have learned to expect it. Stoneguard who leave their holdings often do so as mercenaries, wanderers, or individuals who could not abide the direction their war-band was heading.

Lineage: Veildrift — the Hauntborn
No one knows where the Veildrift came from. The oldest records describe them appearing at the edges of battlefields, in the ruins of collapsed towers, and at sites where large amounts of magic were spent in catastrophic ways. Current scholars debate whether they are the descendants of people who survived a planar boundary event, or something stranger still. The Veildrift themselves rarely discuss it, either because they don’t know or because they have decided it doesn’t matter.
Veildrift are profoundly unsettling in appearance. Their skin is so pale as to be nearly translucent — in dim light, the faint shadows of veins and underlying structure are visible beneath the surface, giving them a ghostly, half-present quality. Their hair is white or silver without exception, and their eyes are a uniform pale blue or silver-grey with no visible pupil. Despite their fragile appearance, they move with unusual precision, and their strange partial-presence makes them naturally difficult to perceive in low light.

The Murrak are a people of disputed origin. Some scholars claim they are the surviving descendants of a civilization that bargained poorly with something ancient. What they lack in raw intellectual processing they compensate for with an instinctive resonance with magical force: Murrak spellcasters often outperform their academic credentials, drawing on something in their blood that instruction alone does not explain.

V. The Beastborn Peoples
The Beastborn peoples are non-human in evolutionary origin. The Keth and Verdathi both have rich civilizations — simply different in structure from the towered cities and trade empires most people think of when they hear the word.
Lineage: Bovari — the Ox-folk
The Bovari are a large and immediately distinctive people: broadly built at the shoulder, standing just under six feet in most cases, with the squared heads and prominent curved horns of bovine ancestry. Both males and females bear horns, and Bovari culture attaches enormous personal significance to them — horns are cultivated, carved, painted, and adorned across a lifetime, and their condition and decoration communicate more about a Bovari’s personal history than any word of introduction.
The most immediately unusual feature of Bovari anatomy is their arms. Each arm bifurcates at the elbow, branching into two forearms and hands. A Bovari at rest typically holds two of their four hands at their sides and keeps the other two available for tasks; in conversation, all four are often in motion. This gives them a social expressiveness that other peoples can find overwhelming, and a practical capability that makes them sought-after as craftspeople, builders, and cooks. Bovari live in nomadic family groups or integrate comfortably into larger settlements, and they are generally regarded as among the most good-natured peoples in Aethermoor — a reputation they accept without particular pride.

Lineage: Naukin — the Ratfolk
The Naukin are ratfolk — lean, quick, whisker-faced, and collectively the product of centuries of exploitation at the hands of mage-scholars who used them as subjects, test cases, servants, and occasionally ingredients. Their escape from the great wizard academies is a matter of historical record; how they organized it is not, because the Naukin have never discussed it with outsiders.
Naukin colonies exist in the cracks of the world: old sewer networks beneath cities, collapsed buildings in the scholars’ districts they once served, tunnels beneath mage towers. They are neither friendly nor hostile to the outside world by default — they are cautious in the extreme. A Naukin encountered alone has almost certainly been sent out deliberately, and a Naukin settlement does not welcome unannounced visitors. Individual Naukin who travel the wider world do so against cultural pressure, driven by necessity, exile, or a curiosity their people regard with suspicion.
Naukin as a group are deeply, institutionally opposed to magic. This is not superstition — it is experiential. Most know exactly what was done to their ancestors with spells and enchantments. Naukin characters who practice magic are extraordinarily rare and face significant social consequences within their communities.

Chapter 13: Social Interaction
Not every problem ends at sword-point. Social systems give non-combat characters real tools and meaningful stakes — and give combat characters reasons to invest in at least one social skill.
Disposition
Every significant NPC has a Disposition toward each character, rated 0–100. This score represents how favorably they regard that character right now.

Making a Speechcraft Check
When a character attempts to persuade, deceive, intimidate, or otherwise influence an NPC, the GM calls for a Speechcraft roll. The target number is the character’s Speechcraft skill plus their Luck Bonus, modified by the NPC’s Disposition and any relevant circumstantial modifiers from the table above.
Success does not mean the NPC does whatever you want. It means you moved them — they were persuaded, won over, convinced, or at minimum placed in a position where refusing would feel inconsistent with who they are. The Disposition score reflects how far that movement can go.
Outcomes by Disposition
The same Speechcraft result produces meaningfully different outcomes depending on how the NPC felt about you before the roll. Two examples below walk through a single scenario — a party attempting to convince a city official to grant access to a restricted archive — played out at two different Disposition levels, with both a success and a failure for each.
Scenario: Gaining Access to a Restricted Archive
FRIENDLY NPC (Disposition 75) — SPEECHCRAFT SUCCESS
The official, Archivist Selenne, already knows the party by reputation and has no particular objection to helping them. She smiles as you make your case. The Speechcraft roll succeeds.
OUTCOME: Selenne doesn’t just grant access — she walks you to the restricted section herself, dismisses the usual waiting period, and mentions a secondary collection that isn’t on the public register. ‘If you’re researching what I think you’re researching,’ she says quietly, ‘there’s a sealed box in the sub-vault that nobody’s opened since the last Archivist died. I’ve been waiting for someone to ask the right question.’
A friendly NPC who is successfully persuaded often goes beyond the minimum. They may share information you didn’t ask for, provide a contact, or take a personal risk on your behalf.
FRIENDLY NPC (Disposition 75) — SPEECHCRAFT FAILURE
Selenne listens patiently, but the roll fails. Perhaps you pushed a detail she couldn’t overlook, or perhaps the request genuinely exceeds what she’s allowed to do alone.
OUTCOME: She frowns apologetically. ‘I want to help. Genuinely. But I can’t authorize restricted access without the head archivist’s seal, and she’s in the capital until next month. What I can do’ — she pauses, lowers her voice — ‘is write you a provisional letter of inquiry and tell you which stacks the material would likely be shelved in. That’s not the same as access, but a clever reader might find what they need without it.’
A friendly NPC who cannot be persuaded still wants to help. They look for alternatives, soften the refusal with useful information, or point the party toward another path. The failure moves the fiction forward, just not through the front door.
UNFRIENDLY NPC (Disposition 25) — SPEECHCRAFT SUCCESS
Senior Registrar Thalwick finds the party irritating and their request presumptuous. He has made this clear twice already. The Speechcraft roll succeeds, but barely.
OUTCOME: Thalwick doesn’t look at you when he speaks. ‘Fine. The archivist on duty tonight will receive a note. You get two hours and you return everything exactly as you found it. If I hear one complaint from her, I revoke it.’ He slides a form across the desk without looking up. ‘Fill this out. Both sides.’
An unfriendly NPC who is successfully persuaded does the bare minimum and makes you feel it. They comply, but slowly, grudgingly, and with conditions. They’re not your ally — you just found the exact right lever to move them this once. Their Disposition does not improve.
UNFRIENDLY NPC (Disposition 25) — SPEECHCRAFT FAILURE
Thalwick has been suspicious of the party since they arrived. The roll fails, and badly.
OUTCOME: He stands up slowly. ‘You’ve been asking about the restricted archives twice now, and you’re not credentialed researchers, not city officials, and not on any visitor register I’ve seen.’ He reaches for a small bell on his desk. ‘I think you should speak with the Watch. I’m sure they’d love to hear what you’re so eager to find.’
An unfriendly NPC whose roll failed may lie (pointing the party to false information or a dead end), alert authorities, raise alarm, or — at Disposition 0–20 — immediately become hostile. The exact response depends on who they are and what they stand to gain. A corrupt official might demand a bribe to stay quiet. A loyal guard might draw their weapon.
CRITICAL FAILURE (natural 98–99): The NPC’s Disposition drops by 10. The lie you told was obvious, the intimidation backfired, or the deception insults their intelligence. At Hostile Disposition, a critical failure may prompt an immediate attack.
Changing Disposition
Disposition is not fixed. It shifts based on what the party does, not just what they say. Actions speak louder than Speechcraft rolls.
IMPROVING DISPOSITION: Completing favors, fulfilling promises, demonstrating shared values, providing useful information, or spending time building a genuine relationship (see Establish Connections in the Downtime section) all raise Disposition over time. A successful Speechcraft roll might raise it by 5. A genuinely transformative action might raise it by 15–20.
LOWERING DISPOSITION: Breaking promises, being caught in a lie, acting against the NPC’s interests, insulting their faction, or failing dramatically with a social roll all lower Disposition. The GM sets the drop. Most failures cost 5–10. Betrayals cost 20–40 or set it to 0 permanently.
Social Skills Beyond Speechcraft
Speechcraft covers persuasion, deception, and intimidation — but social navigation is broader than that.
MERCANTILE: Used for haggling, appraising value, reading commercial intent, and negotiating trade deals. Where Speechcraft is about moving people, Mercantile is about moving numbers.
STREETWISE (Improvised, use Mercantile or Speechcraft at GM discretion): Finding the right person in a city, learning who to bribe, or understanding the local power structure. Characters from urban backgrounds may attempt these at −10 difficulty.
INTIMIDATION AS SPEECHCRAFT: Threatening NPCs falls under Speechcraft but uses END or STR as an auxiliary modifier for the fiction. A physically imposing character threatening a merchant works differently than a charming diplomat suggesting consequences. The roll is the same — only the narrative differs.
Chapter 14: Gods, Pantheons & Religion
The gods of Aethermoor are not myths. They are not entirely absent, either. They are something in between — vast intelligences that shaped the world in an age before memory, whose attention now flickers across centuries, and whose worshippers argue endlessly about what they actually want. Religion in Scrolls & Steel is a source of power, community, faction tension, and some of the game's most interesting moral questions.
The Nine Pillars — The Dominant Pantheon
The Nine Pillars are the gods most commonly worshipped in the lowland kingdoms and trade-city cultures. Their temples are the institutions most adventurers encounter, and their priests the most common source of healing magic.
The Nine Pillars are, in the broad theology of the continent, understood to be a cooperative arrangement — gods who do not conflict, who have divided dominion between themselves as much by temperament as by design. In practice, their clergy argue constantly.

A Priest of Lira-Sun who tends the sick does so because she believes the work is sacred, not because she has had a vision.
In play, the Nine Pillars are most present as institutions: the Auren temple that administers local justice, the Thal-Vor priests who run the city's mortuary records, the Lira-Sun hospice at the edge of the merchants' quarter. Characters interact with the Pillars through their temples and clergy, rarely through direct divine intervention.
The Compact — The Older Powers
Nobody knows where the Compact came from. Not the scholars who study them, not the priests who serve them, and apparently not the Nine Pillars, whose eldest theological texts treat the Compact as simply already present when the world was new — like a draft or a deep smell in an old building that has always been there. The Compact is not a pantheon in the organized sense. It is a loose collection of beings who made agreements with each other and with the world long before mortal civilization had opinions about such things, and who have never seen fit to explain themselves.
Worshippers of the Compact timidly beseech their gods for aid, hoping for a blessing, knowing fully they could be cursed simply for asking. The Compact pantheon is fickle in mortal terms — not malicious, not uncaring, but operating on a scale that makes human urgency seem like weather. Prayers to the Compact are offered at standing stones, in deep caves, at the edges of forests, and at the moments before decisions that cannot be undone.

The Vaeln — The Eldest Court
If the Nine Pillars are the gods of civilization and the Compact the gods of the deep world, then the Vaeln are something else entirely — older than either, stranger than both, and apparently indifferent to the distinction. The Vaeln are a court of fifty fey beings utterly alien to the other inhabitants of Aethermoor and whose origins predate the continent itself. They have been worshipped, feared, bargained with, and mostly misunderstood for as long as there have been people to do any of those things.
No mortal theology has ever satisfactorily explained the Vaeln. Their individual domains are varied and frequently esoteric: one presides over the moment before a decision is made; another over the specific texture of old paper; a third over the migration patterns of fish that no longer exist. Their system of morality operates on principles that scholars have spent careers attempting to map and failed. What has been established, through careful comparative study of the agreements the Vaeln have honored and violated across recorded history, is that two things appear to matter to them above all else: equality among those present, and the keeping of promises.
The Vaeln’s internal hierarchy is in permanent, deliberate flux. There are fifty defined roles within their court — positions of leadership, mid-court standing, and what might be translated as the ‘despised’ or ‘lowest’ — but no individual Vaeln holds any role permanently. Because every member of the Vaeln has lived, died, and reincarnated countless times, and because they are truly ancient, each of the fifty has occupied every role many times over. A being currently at the apex of the court was at its nadir a century ago and will be again. This makes hierarchy meaningless to them in the way it is meaningful to shorter-lived beings, and makes their behavior from any given position radically inconsistent by mortal standards.
Worshipping the Vaeln is not precisely possible in the way the Nine Pillars or Compact can be worshipped. The Vaeln do not require or particularly value devotion. What they respond to is correct behavior: honoring agreements to the letter, treating those around you as equals regardless of station, and occasionally — rarely, and always at cost — requesting an audience through the correct ritual forms. Those who have successfully bargained with the Vaeln consistently report that the beings are not cruel but are absolutely literal, and that misunderstanding the terms of an agreement is entirely the mortal party’s problem.

Religion in Mechanics
A character's religion is primarily a roleplay and narrative element. However, the following practical touchpoints apply:
Temple membership (any recognized faith): reduced cost for healing services. Free minor healing for initiates.
Priestly characters (Vitalism as Major skill): once per long rest, spend 10 minutes in prayer to recover WIL ÷ 20 MP (round down) in addition to standard recovery.
Blasphemy and abandonment: the GM may impose social penalties (Disposition losses with members of the relevant faith) for characters who publicly violate their religious commitments.
RELIGION IN PLAY
Religion works best in Scrolls & Steel as a source of faction tension, personal motivation, and moral questions — not as a power system with alignment strings attached.
A devout Crusader of Auren who encounters a Compact ritual hidden in a noble's cellar has a decision to make. What they decide says something about them. The dice settle what happens next.

Death & Dying
When you reach 0 HP, you fall Unconscious and are Dying. Each round, make a Death Roll: roll d100 against your Endurance. On success, stable this round. On failure, lose 1 more HP.
Death Threshold: You die when you reach negative HP equal to END ÷ 10 (round down). At END 55, death at −5 HP.
An ally can stabilize you by spending their Action to: use a healing spell on you, apply a Restore Health potion, or succeed on a Vitalism skill roll (stops HP loss; you stay at 0 and Unconscious until healed).
DEATH & DYING EXAMPLE
Oryn has END 55. Death threshold: 55 ÷ 10 = 5. He dies at −5 HP.
Round 1: Oryn drops to 0 HP. Rolls d100 vs END 55, gets 48. Success — stable.
Round 2: No ally can reach him. He rolls 72. Failure — drops to −1 HP.
Round 3: He rolls 61. Failure — drops to −2 HP.
Round 4: He rolls 29. Success — stable at −2 HP.
Round 5: An ally reaches him. Uses Restore Health (Minor): heals 1d6+1. Rolls 4+1 = 5. Oryn goes from −2 HP to +3 HP. He regains consciousness and can act next round.
If Oryn had reached −5 HP before being reached, he would have died.
His allies had several rounds to respond. This window — tense, finite, real — is intentional.
Chapter 16: Terrain, Environment & Exploration
The world of Aethermoor is not a flat gray floor. Dungeons have pits. Battlefields have rain and fire. Coastal ruins flood. Mountains kill the unprepared. This chapter collects rules for interacting with the physical environment, light, and the elements.
Difficult Terrain
Difficult terrain includes: deep mud, shallow water, rubble, steep slopes, thick undergrowth, ice, and similar obstacles. Moving through difficult terrain costs double movement. A character with a normal move of 20m moves only 10m per turn through difficult terrain.
EXAMPLES: Thick mud costs double movement and may require an Athletics check (Normal) to avoid being slowed. Icy surfaces cost double movement and require an Acrobatics check (Normal) or the character falls Prone. Dense undergrowth costs double movement for non-Forest-Born races.
Difficult terrain does not affect creatures that are immune to it (e.g., flying creatures, certain supernatural entities, Arantza climbing on walls above it).
Environmental Hazards and Created Effects
Terrain can be altered by combat. These rules cover the most common effects:
BURNING GROUND: A Fireball or similar fire spell hitting a flammable surface (dry wood, oil, dry grass) can ignite a zone (~5m radius). Each round, any creature standing in burning ground takes 1d6 fire damage at the start of their turn (no AR applies). Burning ground is also difficult terrain. A burning zone typically persists for 1d6 rounds before burning out, or until doused.
GREASE / SLICK GROUND: Alchemical grease, ice spells, or magical effects can create a slick patch (up to 5m radius). Creatures entering or moving in a slick zone must make an Acrobatics check (Normal difficulty). On failure, they fall Prone and their movement ends. Getting up from Prone costs their next Action.
SMOKE AND OBSCUREMENT: Dense smoke, fog, or magical darkness creates heavily obscured areas. All attack rolls targeting creatures in or through an obscured area are at Disadvantage. Night-Eye does not help against smoke or mundane fog — only darkness.
Cover (Variant Rule)
This variant applies when the party is in an environment with meaningful cover (ruins, barricades, overturned furniture, boulders).
HALF COVER (behind a low wall, barrel, or partial obstruction): Ranged attacks against you suffer +10 to the roll. You must spend your movement to duck behind or emerge from cover.
FULL COVER (entirely hidden behind a wall, inside a doorway, or below a parapet): You cannot be targeted by ranged attacks or spells that require line of sight. You have no line of sight to attack from this position either — you must move into partial cover or open ground first.
Falling
A character who falls from a height takes Crushing damage based on the distance fallen.
Falling damage: 1d6 per 5 meters fallen, rounded up, to a maximum of 10d6 (at 30m or more). This is Crushing damage and is not reduced by AR. The Slowfall spell (Alteration, 1 MP) negates all falling damage.
LANDING ROLL: A character who is aware of the fall and has at least 1 FP remaining may make an Acrobatics check (Normal). On a success, reduce the falling damage by 1d6 (to a minimum of 0).
FALLING INTO WATER: Falling into deep water from a height of 15m or less deals no damage. Above 15m, treat as falling onto solid ground but halve the distance for damage calculation. (if falling from 40m above the water, calculate it as though falling onto solid ground from 20m)
Light and Darkness
Light conditions matter in dungeons, forests at night, and underground environments.
BRIGHT LIGHT: Normal conditions. No penalties to sight-dependent checks.
DIM LIGHT (torchlight edge, cloudy day, moonlit night): Checks relying on sight are at +10 difficulty. Ranged attacks beyond 15m are at +10 to roll.
DARKNESS (no light source, deep underground, magically darkened area): All attack rolls targeting creatures in darkness are at Disadvantage. All sight-based skill checks are at +20. Creatures without Night-Eye, darkvision, or a racial ability cannot target specific creatures — they must guess their location.
LIGHT SOURCES: A torch illuminates a 5m radius of bright light and an additional 5m of dim light, lasting approximately 1 hour. A lantern illuminates 10m bright / 10m dim, lasting 4 hours per flask of oil. The Illusion spell Light provides 5m bright light until next rest.
NIGHT-EYE: The Night-Eye spell (Illusion) or equivalent racial ability allows the character to see in darkness as if it were dim light. It does not help in smoke or magical darkness.
Underwater Exploration and Combat
Most characters can hold their breath for a number of rounds equal to END ÷ 10 (round down, minimum 2). After this, they must make an END check each round (Normal, escalating +10 per round after the first failure). On a failed check, the character begins drowning: they drop to 0 HP at the end of that round regardless of current HP and begin dying normally.
The Water Breathing spell (Alteration) or aquatic racial abilities remove this limitation entirely.
UNDERWATER MOVEMENT: Swimming is treated as difficult terrain (half movement) for non-aquatic characters. Characters without a swimming racial ability or Athletics 50+ must make Athletics checks to maintain position in currents or rough water (Normal for calm, Hard for rough).
UNDERWATER COMBAT: Non-aquatic characters fighting underwater suffer the following penalties: all weapon attacks at Disadvantage except daggers, short blades, and spears; ranged attacks (bows, crossbows) are impossible; spells that require arm motion (most Elementalism, Alteration spells) are at +20 difficulty. Crushing damage from blunt weapons is halved underwater.
Aquatic creatures and characters with appropriate racial abilities (Verdathi, Kreln, Verdathi) fight underwater without penalty and have Advantage on all attacks against non-aquatic creatures in water.
Mounted Combat
A mounted character uses their mount’s movement speed instead of their own. The mount acts on the rider’s initiative. Mounts cannot use a Full Move and allow the rider to attack in the same turn — the rider moves and attacks normally (no +10 attack penalty for movement while mounted on a trained mount).
TRAINED MOUNTS: A mount trained for combat does not flee from the sight of combat. Untrained mounts require a Speechcraft or Athletics check (Normal) to maintain control during combat. On a failure, the mount bolts or bucks, counting as forced movement for the rider.
CHARGING: A mounted character who moves at least 10m in a straight line before attacking may add 1d6 bonus damage to one melee attack. This damage increases to 1d12 at 20m. The attack is made at Normal difficulty (no penalty). This can only be used once per combat unless the rider moves away from the target and charges again.
DISMOUNTING: Forced dismount (being knocked off, mount killed) requires an Acrobatics check (Normal). On failure, the rider falls Prone and may take falling damage (GM discretion based on height and mount movement).
TARGETING MOUNTS: Enemies may choose to attack the mount rather than the rider. If the mount reaches 0 HP, it collapses, potentially forcing a dismount check.
Shouts: Speechcraft in Combat (Optional Rule)
For groups who want Speechcraft to have a combat role, this optional rule adds battlefield presence effects. These are not spells and cost no MP — they rely on voice, will, and the weight of the words themselves.
Any character may attempt a Shout as their Action once per combat. Roll Speechcraft (Normal difficulty). On a success, choose one effect:
WAR CRY: All allies within 15m who can hear you gain +5 to their next attack roll this round only.
INTIMIDATE: One enemy within 10m must make a WIL check (Normal). On failure, that enemy is Frightened for 1 round.
COMMANDING SHOUT: One ally within 10m may immediately make a free Reaction attack (does not use their Reaction for blocking). This interrupts the current turn order.
Characters with Speechcraft as a Major skill may attempt this once per short rest (not once per combat), reflecting professional mastery of presence. The Bard’s archetype bonus of substituting Speechcraft for adjacent skills also applies to Shouts.
Appendix A: Weapons
Each weapon lists two damage values: Primary and Secondary. Primary is the weapon's optimal attack method. Secondary is an alternate grip or strike — lower, reflecting the weapon is less suited to that motion. Declare which you are using before rolling. Both use the same weapon skill.
Example: A steel greatsword deals 1d10+1 Slashing (sweeping cut) or 1d8+1 Piercing (thrusting with the point). You roll Long Blade for both.

PIERCING  — Puncturing damage. Finds gaps in heavy armor. Targeted piercing ignores 1 AR.
CRUSHING  — Impact damage. Best against armored, bone-shelled, or undead targets.
REACH     — Attack from one zone back without entering enemy's melee space.
THROWN    — Can be thrown at Short range (10m) using Marksman skill.
TWO-HANDED — Requires both hands. Cannot use a shield.
VERSATILE — One-handed or two-handed. Two-handed: +1 damage, no shield allowed.

Appendix E: Quick Reference
CORE RULES — ONE BOX
ROLL: d100 equal to or under (Skill + Luck Bonus). Difficulty adds to your roll.
LUCK BONUS: LCK ÷ 10 (round down). Added to every roll. OR use as Luck Dice pool (variant).
ADVANTAGE: Roll twice, use lower. DISADVANTAGE: Roll twice, use higher.
CRITICAL SUCCESS: Natural 1 or 2 (2%). Always succeeds. Max damage / bonus spell effect.
CRITICAL FAIL: Natural 98 or 99 (2%). Always fails. Something goes wrong.
FATIGUE COSTS: Light weapon 1 FP. Medium 2 FP. Heavy 3 FP. Ranged 1 FP. Spell 1 FP. Round passive 1 FP.
FP = (END + STR + AGI + SPD) ÷ 10.
0 FP = Staggered (+10 all rolls, no spells, all attacks at Disadvantage). Damaged while Staggered = Knocked Down.
ONE-HANDED + SHIELD: +5 to attack rolls.
ARMOR: Flat reduction. Armor skill bonus: 25–49=+1, 50–74=+2, 75–99=+3, 100=+4.
BLOCK: Declare before attacker rolls (Reaction, once per round). If hit lands, roll Block: success = halve damage after AR. Wasted if attack would have missed (Practiced Guard feat recovers this once per combat).
MAGICKA: Spend MP + 1 FP to cast. Roll magic school. Fail = MP and FP spent, no effect.
MP Recovery: Short rest = WIL÷10. Long rest = all.
SKILL ADVANCE: Tick when used meaningfully. On long rest: d100. If result > skill, gain 1d4.
LEVEL UP: Every 10 Major Skill increases. Gain: +1d4+1 to 3 Attrs, +2 HP, +1 MP, 1 Feat.
Every 5 levels: gain a Boon.
MOVEMENT: SPD ÷ 2 (round to nearest 5) meters per turn. Racial minimums apply.
RANGE: Touch 2m | Short 10m | Long 30m | Very Long 60m | Extreme 120m
DYING: At 0 HP, roll END each round. Fail = −1 HP. Die at −(END ÷ 10) HP.
EQUIPMENT DEGRADATION: d10 after each combat. On 1: drop one condition tier.
DISEASE: Max FP −10% per stage. Penalties double at Days 4, 8, 15.
